# Apple-Product-Pricing-2020-2026

## Overview
- The dataset includes 80K pricing snapshots across 31 Apple models, with an average observed price of $782.77, an average discount of 21.42%, and a strong average rating of 4.5/5. 
Product availability appears generally stable, with approximately 69% of observations classified as In Stock and an Out-of-Stock rate of only 16.84%, indicating relatively low exposure to stock shortages and potential lost sales. However, the 11K Low Stock observations may signal supply pressure for selected products or promotional periods. 
Prices remain broadly stable over time, while discounts show stronger seasonal peaks, particularly around Q4. 
Premium MacBook Pro configurations dominate the highest-priced products, led by the MacBook Pro 14-inch M4 Pro 512GB at an average observed price of $1,827.

## Product Lyfecycle Pricing
- Apple products experience relatively slow price erosion during the early lifecycle but accelerate noticeably after the first year. The average discount increases from only 7.08% during the first 6 months to 33.44% after 25 months—an increase of 26.36 percentage points (approximately 3.7×). This suggests that aggressive promotions are concentrated in the late lifecycle rather than immediately after launch.
- The average observed selling price declines from approximately $900 in months 0–12 to around $600 after month 37, representing a reduction of roughly one-third of product value. Maintaining premium pricing during the first 12 months therefore preserves substantially more revenue per transaction than applying early discounts.
- The median observed month to the first 20% discount is 0. Among models that eventually reached a 20% discount, at least half were already discounted by 20% or more in their first observed month. Because product age is measured from the model’s first appearance in the dataset rather than its official launch date, this result should be interpreted as an observed-data milestone, not as evidence of immediate post-launch discounting.
Watch and iPad recorded the strongest price erosion in month 36, with price index values ​​of 61.77 and 64.62 respectively, while the 3-month rolling discount reached 40.04% and 35.10%. Conversely, Mac maintained the best price retention, with a price index of 79.52 and a 3-month rolling discount of only 21.95%. By month 36, the Watch's price erosion was approximately 17.75 index points higher than the Mac's, and the iPad's was approximately 14.90 index points higher. => This result supports a differentiated lifecycle pricing strategy by category: continue maintaining premium pricing for Mac for longer, while the Watch and iPad should be moved to a higher-intensity promotional phase sooner to limit uncontrolled price erosion.
=>Mac products maintain higher lifecycle price indices than other categories, suggesting stronger pricing power. In contrast, Watch and iPad enter a steeper markdown phase after roughly 18–24 months. A differentiated lifecycle pricing strategy—maintaining premium pricing for Mac while increasing promotional intensity for Watch and iPad during the late lifecycle—is likely to improve pricing efficiency without applying uniform discounts across all product categories.
## Sale Event Effectiveness
• Promotional events increase the average discount from 20.30% during non-event periods to 33.71% during event periods, producing an Event Discount Lift of 13.41 percentage points. This confirms that major campaigns substantially strengthen promotional intensity compared with normal pricing.

• Big Billion Days delivers the strongest promotional impact with an Event Discount Lift of 16.72 percentage points, outperforming Black Friday (11.16%) by 5.56 percentage points. For product categories such as iPhone and Mac, prioritizing inventory and marketing spend during Big Billion Days is likely to generate greater promotional impact than allocating the same resources evenly across all events.

• Event participation remains relatively limited, with Event Coverage at only 8.31%. This means that more than 90% of pricing observations occur outside promotional campaigns. Expanding event participation selectively for high-performing categories while preserving margin controls could significantly increase promotional reach without requiring additional event types.

• Promotional effectiveness varies considerably across product categories. iPhone records the highest average event lift (15.49%), whereas iPad achieves only 11.30%. Rather than applying uniform discount policies, event budgets should be allocated according to category responsiveness. Increasing investment in higher-performing categories is expected to improve overall promotional efficiency while reducing unnecessary discount expenditure on lower-performing categories.

• Platform differences indicate that Big Billion Days performs particularly well on Flipkart, whereas Black Friday shows more balanced performance between platforms. Platform-specific campaign planning should therefore be preferred over identical cross-platform discount strategies, allowing each marketplace to focus on the events where its historical promotional response has been strongest.
